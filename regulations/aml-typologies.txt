Money Laundering Typologies and Red-Flag Indicators

Source: Synthetic summary of common AML typologies aligned with FATF and FinCEN
guidance. For training/demo use only; not legal advice.

1. The three stages of money laundering
- Placement: introducing illicit cash into the financial system (e.g. cash deposits,
  buying monetary instruments, co-mingling with a cash-intensive business).
- Layering: moving funds through a series of transactions to obscure their origin
  (e.g. wire transfers between accounts and jurisdictions, shell companies, conversion
  into and out of assets).
- Integration: returning laundered funds to the criminal as apparently legitimate
  wealth (e.g. property purchases, business investments, loans to oneself).

2. Common typologies
- Smurfing / structuring: many small cash transactions kept below reporting thresholds.
- Money mules: third parties who receive and forward funds, often recruited online,
  used to distance the criminal from the money. Mule networks frequently appear in the
  transaction graph as rings or fan-in/fan-out patterns around a hub account.
- Funnel accounts: cash deposited in many locations and rapidly withdrawn elsewhere.
- Trade-based money laundering: mis-invoicing, over/under-shipment, or phantom shipments
  to move value across borders under cover of trade.
- Rapid movement / pass-through: funds that arrive and leave an account within a short
  window, leaving little or no balance.

3. Behavioural and transactional red flags
- A new account that immediately receives and forwards large sums (pass-through).
- A web of accounts transacting in a closed loop (a ring), suggesting layering.
- Transactions with no apparent economic or lawful purpose.
- Activity inconsistent with the customer's known occupation, income, or history.
- Sudden activity from a dormant account, or transactions just under reporting limits.
- Reluctance to provide identification or beneficial-ownership information.

4. Investigator response
When these indicators appear, the analyst gathers the transaction history, examines the
account's neighbourhood in the graph for rings or mule structures, checks the relevant
reporting obligations, and — where suspicion is established — drafts a suspicious activity
or suspicious transaction report for review and filing.
