FINTRAC — Suspicious Transaction Reporting and Structuring (Canada)

Source: Synthetic summary of Canada's Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and
Terrorist Financing Act (PCMLTFA) and FINTRAC guidance. For training/demo use only;
not legal advice and not a verbatim reproduction of any official text.

1. Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs)
Reporting entities (banks, money services businesses, casinos, and other regulated
persons) must submit a Suspicious Transaction Report to FINTRAC when there are
reasonable grounds to suspect that a transaction — or an attempted transaction — is
related to the commission or attempted commission of a money laundering offence or a
terrorist activity financing offence. There is no minimum dollar threshold for an STR:
even a small or attempted transaction must be reported if suspicion is established.
STRs must be filed as soon as practicable after the reporting entity has taken measures
that enabled it to establish that suspicion.

2. Large Cash Transaction Reports (LCTRs)
Separately from STRs, a reporting entity must file a Large Cash Transaction Report when
it receives CAD 10,000 or more in cash in a single transaction, or when it receives two
or more cash amounts totalling CAD 10,000 or more within a 24-hour period from the same
person or entity (the "24-hour rule"). The LCTR is threshold-based and is required even
when there is no suspicion.

3. Structuring
Structuring (also called smurfing) is the deliberate breaking up of a large amount of
cash or value into multiple smaller transactions specifically to stay below a reporting
threshold such as the CAD 10,000 large-cash threshold, or to otherwise avoid detection.
Structuring is itself a strong indicator of money laundering. When a reporting entity
detects a pattern consistent with structuring — for example, repeated cash deposits just
under CAD 10,000, deposits split across branches, accounts, or short time windows, or a
customer asking how to avoid a report — it has reasonable grounds to suspect money
laundering and MUST file a Suspicious Transaction Report, regardless of the fact that no
individual transaction reached the LCTR threshold.

What must be reported for structuring under FINTRAC: file a Suspicious Transaction Report
(STR) describing the suspected structuring activity. If any single cash transaction or
24-hour aggregate still reaches CAD 10,000, the corresponding Large Cash Transaction
Report is also required. Reporting entities must keep records supporting the report.

4. Red flags commonly associated with structuring
- Multiple cash deposits or withdrawals just below CAD 10,000.
- The same person conducting cash transactions at several branches the same day.
- A customer who appears to be acting on behalf of someone else, or who is reluctant
  to provide identification when a threshold is approached.
- Transactions broken into round-number amounts that, in aggregate, exceed thresholds.
